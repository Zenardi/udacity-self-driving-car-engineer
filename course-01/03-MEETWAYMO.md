# What is Waymo?

Waymo is an autonomous driving technology company witha mission to make it safe and easy to get people and things where they're going.We were founded in 2009 as the Google self-driving car project.Since then, we have autonomously driven tens of millions of mileson the railroads and tens of billions of miles in simulation.We're the first company to have a commercial 100 percentfully autonomous ride-hailing service on public roads, Waymo One,anyone in our service territory,which is currently in Chandler, Arizona,can download our app and haila fully autonomous Waymo vehicle and experience the truly driverless experience.We also see other use cases for the driver,including autonomous trucking, and delivery,and eventually personal car ownership.

First, we test the Waymo driver,which is what we call our autonomous driving technology out in the real-world.At the time this was recorded,we have autonomous driven more than 20 million miles onpublic roads in dozens of cities in the United States. We also tested our numerous golf course facilities which had been speciallydesigned and redesigned over the years for Waymo zone, unique testing needs.Last but not least,the Waymo driver also drives in simulation,where we have autonomously driven tens of billions of miles,validating and testing our technologies behavior before it even hits the road.

Waymo is tackling some of the hardest problems that exist inmachine learning and is a unique position to contribute tothe research community with one ofthe largest and most diverse autonomous driving datasets ever released.The way open dataset is always changing.As of March 2021,it consists of a perception dataset and a motion dataset.The perception dataset contains high-resolution,lighter and camera data collected by our vehicles,annotated both as 3D and 2D boxes for objects like vehicles and pedestrians,and also bicyclists and signs.The motion dataset consists of more than 100,000 segments,which corresponds to 570 hours of unique data withspecifically mindful scenes that containinteresting interactions and provide object trajectories and 3D maps for each scene.Both datasets feature a wide variety of road types anddriving condition captured aroundthe clock in different urban environments such as San Francisco,Phoenix, Mountain View, Range in Seattle and more.We did this to encourage research into modelsthat can better generalize to new driving environments.

## Objects in the Waymo Open Dataset
We chose to label vehicle, cyclists,and pedestrians in the open data set because these are some ofthe most important objects that require understanding and perception abilities.They also cover interesting range of very common objects such as car vehicles, cars,and rarer objects such as motorcycles or bicycles,and this allows you to test your methods in both regimes.

# Why is Radar Omitted in the Waymo Open Dataset?
When we created the dataset,we focused on light and cameras.These are the primary sensors the 3D perception researchers use.We believe those are sufficient to capturethe variety of potential representations in applications we're facing.I think radar can be extended to be doing similar ones,but we were not able to include it at the time.

# The Most Important Techniques for Autonomous Vehicles
Different types of learning are applicable for self-driving cars.First of all, supervised learning is the standard main stain in the industry,it works really well.You can find it through out the stack,especially popular in perception and prediction,but also in different modules in planning.Other types of learning that are becoming popular areimitation learning and reinforcement or inverse reinforcement learning.They come into play in behavior prediction and planning.When you want to develop systems that maximize a certain set of behavior or rewards overa longer-term horizon potentially working in what we call enclosed loop settingwhere the system needs to perform a certain activity over a period of time.Also, recently, another type of learning has become very popular,this is self-supervised learning.This is the ability of models to bootstrap themselves without explicit labelslearning representations that generalize to task of interest with less data.That is really exciting and has the potential to revolutionize perceptionnot to require hundreds of millions of labeled boxes, for example.Another type of learning is generative model learning.This includes models such as variational autoencoders and GANs.Such model is also very useful in certain tasks,for example, perceptual realism,generating data or sensor inputs that are difficult to tell apart from the original,even in the synthesized environment.These are some examples that come to mind.

# Prediction in Autonomous Vehicles
Prediction is an area humans have long been better at than computers when it comes to driving - have you ever felt sure a car in front of you was going to change lanes, even without their blinker on?

On this page, Dragomir will discuss prediction in autonomous vehicles, and how Waymo is closing the gap (or even exceeding!) human ability in the area.

In many cases, our vehicle needs to predictover 10-15 seconds into the future in order to plan a complex maneuver,such as an unprotected left turn that could take a while to execute.

## Techniques that Work Well for Prediction
There is a variety of techniques that work well for prediction. Some of the more classical methods that combinemotion planning and machine learning work quite well andencode well the knowledge that humans have about constraints in the environment.However, recently, it's beenan incredibly hot field to keep improving the end-to-end behavior prediction models.Those that take in representation of the environment,whether it from sensor data or from an intermediate perception representation,and directly give you the full behavior prediction output,and those are now really good as well.There's multiple ways in which it can be done well nowadays.

## Data Structures used for Prediction
There is an open question of what are the most effective data structures for prediction.When thinking about prediction,one needs to think about how to represent agents,actions and history, as well as the semantic context of the scene,the current map, and what is contained in it.Our understanding and the community's understanding ofthose has been changing and improving.For example, you can model agents as bounding boxes.That leads to certain quality of prediction.However, understanding the gaze and gestures andactions of people in the environment is oftena big part of accurate renavigating the scene.In map representations traditionally,what is popular is representing the map asan image and applying convolutional neural networks to this top-down image.Recently, there is a lot of work that's representing map in a structured way.This includes our vector network that we published at CVPR,where we model the map directly as polylines andfeed it into a neural network that containsa graphical neural net processing transformersand get better results than what you can get by rendering top-down images.There is a trend towards richer,more structured representations ofthe environment and models that can take advantage of that.
